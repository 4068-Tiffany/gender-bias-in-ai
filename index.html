The Problem In Plain English

AI learns from the past.
The past wasn't fair.

📸

Your face isn't treated equally

Facial recognition used in phones, airports, and cameras makes far more mistakes on dark-skinned women than anyone else.

💬 "It's not the camera. It's what the AI was trained on."
💼

AI steers careers

When AI learns from old job data, it picks up old stereotypes that surgeons are men, nurses are women and then recommends jobs the same way.

💬 "Same CV, different name. Very different job suggestions."
🎨

Images shape what we think is normal

Search "CEO" or "doctor" on Google you mostly see men. Even though women make up nearly half of doctors in real life.

💬 "What AI shows shapes what people think is possible."
Module 01 — Facial Recognition

The camera fails
dark-skinned women

We analysed 108,000 face images. Dark-skinned women are significantly underrepresented in the training data so AI performs worst on them.

0.68
Female-to-male ratio for dark-skinned faces. Perfect = 1.0. AI saw far more dark-skinned men than women so it learned them better.
34%
Higher error rate for dark-skinned women in commercial AI systems. This isn't a glitch it's the direct consequence of biased training data.
p<.001
Statistically significant. The imbalance is not random it's a pattern baked into how the data was collected in the first place.

Gender split across skin tone groups darker bars = more men than women

Female-to-male ratio by skin tone below the red line = underrepresented

Module 02 — Job Recommendations

AI thinks surgeons
are always men

We read 257,000 professional biographies. High-paying jobs were mostly about men. Lower-paying jobs mostly women. AI learns this and recommends jobs the same way.

0.41
Bias score (Cramér's V). Anything above 0.3 is a strong bias. Gender and job type are deeply linked in AI training data.
37%
Lower average salary in female-coded professions vs male-coded ones. AI doesn't just learn stereotypes it learns women's work is worth less.
86%
Female in nurse biographies vs just 15% in surgeon biographies. This is what AI sees. This is what it will reproduce.

Each bar shows how male or female-dominated a profession is in AI training data

Average salary for female-coded vs male-coded professions a $44K gap

Module 03 — AI Image Generation

Search "CEO".
See mostly men.

We compared what AI image search actually shows vs real workforce data. The gap is consistent AI systematically erases women from high-status jobs in images.

−18%
Surgeon bias gap. 40% of real surgeons are women. AI shows only 22%. That's 18 women erased from every 100 images of surgeons.
−17%
CEO bias gap. 29% of real CEOs are women. AI shows only 12%. When we only see male CEOs in images, that becomes our expectation.
p<.05
Statistically significant across both Google and Bing. Not a coincidence a consistent, measurable pattern in AI image systems.

Pink = AI shows fewer women than exist in real life. Each bar is one profession.

Points below the dashed line = AI underrepresents women vs reality

So what does
this actually mean?

🔄

It creates a loop

Biased AI outputs become tomorrow's training data. If AI steers women away from senior roles, fewer women appear there in future making the bias worse over time.

👁️

Seeing shapes believing

When we search "doctor" and see mostly men, we unconsciously start to associate leadership with men. AI shapes how we see the world whether we realise it or not.

⚠️

Real women are harmed

Dark-skinned women being misidentified by facial recognition isn't just a technical error. It has led to wrongful arrests, denied access, and security failures.

📊

Data is never neutral

Every dataset reflects the world it was collected from. If that world was unequal — and it was — the AI will be too. Fairness has to be designed in deliberately.

What Should Change

Bias can
be fixed.
But only if
we measure
it first.

These findings are not a reason to distrust AI. They're a call to build it better — with intention, diversity, and accountability.

4 Recommendations

Things that need
to happen now

For AI Builders

Audit before you train

Check who is — and isn't — in your training data before building any model. Overall accuracy hides who the system fails.

For Researchers

Measure intersectionally

Don't measure gender and race separately. Dark-skinned women face both at once. Fairness tools need to keep up.

For Organisations

Hire diverse teams

People with different lived experiences will spot bias a homogeneous team simply won't notice no matter how skilled.

For Everyone

Ask harder questions

When you use AI, ask: who does this work well for? Who might it fail? Accountability starts with asking.

Fair AI is
possible.
But it won't
happen by accident.
